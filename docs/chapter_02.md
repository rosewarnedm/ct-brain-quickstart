## Chapter 2: Normal Brain Anatomy on CT

## Why Tissues Look the Way They Do

CT attenuation reflects tissue electron density. For pattern recognition in the acute setting, remember:

- Grey matter contains more water and protein, less lipid, than white matter — so <⚠️ [#20 REVIEW: ]<grey matter is ~5–10 HU more attenuating than white matter>>.
- CSF is near water but slightly higher due to dissolved solutes.
- Blood density tracks haematocrit; anaemia lowers blood HU.
- Dense skull base bone causes beam hardening — a major source of artefactual hyper- or hypodensity in the posterior fossa.

### Reference HU values (non-contrast)

<⚠️ [#21 REVIEW: ]<- Air: −1000 HU
- Fat: −80 to −120 HU
- CSF: 0–15 HU
- White matter: 20–30 HU
- Grey matter (cortical and deep): 35–45 HU
- Flowing blood: 30–45 HU
- Acute clotted blood: 50–70 HU
- Calcification: >100 HU (often 150–400)
- Cortical bone: >1000 HU>>

These ranges shift with kernel and patient factors — interpret as ranges, not absolutes.

### Windowing

- <⚠️ [#22 REVIEW: ]<Brain window: W 80–100, L 35–45>>
- <⚠️ [#23 REVIEW: ]<Subdural/soft tissue: W 130–150, L 30–35>> — essential for picking up thin extra-axial collections and subtle grey–white loss.
- <⚠️ [#24 REVIEW: ]<Posterior fossa: W 120–160, L 30–40>> with thin slices to mitigate beam hardening.
- <⚠️ [#25 REVIEW: ]<Bone: W 2000–3000, L 300–500>>.

Always adjust windows interactively — fixed defaults miss early ischaemia and small haemorrhages.

---

## Systematic Review

Work through every scan in the same order:

1. Scalp and skull (bone windows).
2. Extra-axial spaces (falx, tentorium, subdural, epidural).
3. Ventricles and CSF pathways.
4. Basal cisterns.
5. Parenchyma — grey–white differentiation, deep grey, insular ribbon, brainstem, cerebellum.
6. Midline structures — septum, pineal, third ventricle.
7. Vessels — arterial calcification, sinus density and calibre.
8. Orbits, sinuses, mastoids, extracranial soft tissues.

---

## Calvarium and Scalp

Symmetric scalp fat. Diploë between cortical tables. Parietal venous lakes are normal — rounded, well-defined, with sclerotic margins.

**Pitfalls:**
- Venous lakes vs lytic lesion: lakes are non-expansile with corticated rims.
- <⚠️ [#26 REVIEW: ]<Accessory sutures vs fractures: sutures are zig-zag, interdigitated, corticated; fractures are sharp, non-corticated, and may cross sutures.>>

---

## Meninges and Extra-axial Spaces

Falx and tentorium are thin hyperattenuating lines; calcification is common with age (sharply marginated, >100 HU). Sulci and Sylvian fissures contain CSF symmetrically.

**Key differential — dural calcification vs acute subdural haemorrhage:**
- <⚠️ [#27 REVIEW: ]<Calcification: >100 HU, sharp margins, conforms to dural reflections, stable on priors.>>
- <⚠️ [#28 REVIEW: ]<Acute subdural: crescentic, 50–70 HU, layers dependently, causes mass effect, crosses sutures but not the falx.>>

---

## Ventricular System

### Normal calibre
- <⚠️ [#29 REVIEW: ]<Adult temporal horns are typically slit-like or <2 mm.>>
- <⚠️ [#30 REVIEW: ]<Third ventricle: ~2–6 mm in adults.>>
- Aqueduct and fourth ventricle midline and patent.

### Quantitative indices
- <⚠️ [#31 REVIEW: ]<Evans index (frontal horn width / inner skull diameter at the same level): normal <0.30.>>
- <⚠️ [#32 REVIEW: ]<Callosal angle (coronal, through posterior commissure): 100–120° normally; lower in NPH.>>

### Normal variants
- **Cavum septum pellucidum / vergae:** midline CSF-density spaces between or behind the frontal horns. No mass effect.
- **Cavum velum interpositum:** CSF-density triangle below the splenium.
- Asymmetric lateral ventricles are common if there's no mass effect.

### Clinical pearl
<⚠️ [#33 REVIEW: ]<Visible temporal horn dilatation (>2–3 mm) is an early and sensitive sign of hydrocephalus>> — don't rely solely on frontal horn size.

---

## Basal Cisterns

Recognise: suprasellar (star-shaped), interpeduncular, ambient, quadrigeminal, prepontine, CP angle, foramen magnum.

**Why this matters:** basal cisterns efface early in raised ICP and diffuse cerebral oedema. Patent cisterns argue strongly against significant global swelling at the time of imaging — and new hyperdensity (50–70 HU) within cisterns implies SAH.

---

## Cerebral Hemispheres

### Grey–white
Cortex 35–45 HU; white matter 20–30 HU. The **insular ribbon** along the Sylvian fissure should be sharply defined — loss is an early MCA infarct sign.

### Deep grey at the foramen of Monro
- Caudate heads: anteromedial to frontal horns.
- Putamen: lateral to internal capsule.
- Globus pallidus: medial to putamen (may calcify finely with age).
- Internal capsule: lower attenuation than adjacent grey nuclei.
- Thalami: paramedian, similar HU to cortex.

**The symmetry trap:** bilateral pathology can look "normal." Always check absolute HU and conspicuity of the insular ribbon and lentiform nuclei.

---

## Brainstem and Posterior Fossa

- Midbrain: "Mickey Mouse" cerebral peduncles, midline aqueduct.
- Pons: ovoid, homogeneous, anterior to fourth ventricle.
- Cerebellum: folia visible; vermis midline.

Beam hardening is the main pitfall — always correlate across slices and adjust windows before calling cerebellar hyper- or hypodensity.

---

## Vessels on Non-contrast CT

### Arteries
Luminal blood ~30–45 HU. Atherosclerotic wall calcification is curvilinear, dense (>100 HU), eccentric, along the carotid siphons, vertebrals and basilar.

**Hyperdense artery sign (acute thrombus) vs calcification:**
- <⚠️ [#34 REVIEW: ]<Thrombus: intraluminal, expansile, ~55–70 HU, denser than the contralateral vessel.>>
- <⚠️ [#35 REVIEW: ]<Calcification: eccentric (mural), >100 HU, often nodular, bone-like on bone windows.>>

### Dural sinuses
Density rises with haematocrit and dehydration without indicating thrombosis. Normal sinuses are not expanded and have smooth margins.

<⚠️ [#36 REVIEW: ]<Suspect sinus thrombosis when there is sinus expansion, a triangular hyperdense "cord sign," and consistent clinical context — confirm with venography.>>

---

## Normal Intracranial Calcifications

- **Pineal:** midline; eccentric or mass-forming calcification suggests tumour.
- **Choroid plexus:** trigonal, often bilateral, dense.
- **Globus pallidus:** fine, symmetric in older adults. Extensive or early/young-patient calcification — particularly involving thalami, dentate, or subcortical white matter — suggests metabolic, infectious or genetic cause.
- **Falx/tentorium:** linear; benign.

<⚠️ [#37 REVIEW: ]<Choroid plexus vs intraventricular haemorrhage: plexus is fixed in the atria and very dense; haemorrhage is 50–70 HU, irregular, and layers dependently — always check the non-dependent parts of the horns.>>

---

## Age-related Appearances

### Neonates and infants
- Open sutures, wide extracerebral CSF spaces can be normal.
- White matter is **relatively hypoattenuating** (high water, low myelin) — do not mistake for oedema.
- Early myelinated tracts (posterior limb of internal capsule, brainstem) appear slightly more attenuating than surrounding unmyelinated white matter.

### Ageing brain
- Sulci and ventricles enlarge with atrophy; temporal horns may become visible without hydrocephalus **provided basal cisterns are preserved and there is no transependymal oedema**.
- Benign calcifications (pineal, plexus, falx) become more prominent.

---

## Common Mimics

- **Cavum septi/vergae/velum interpositum** vs interhemispheric SDH — cava are midline, symmetric, CSF density, no mass effect; SDH is crescentic and may cause shift.
- **Mega cisterna magna** vs posterior fossa arachnoid cyst — mega CM communicates freely, no scalloping or mass effect.
- **Prominent perivascular spaces** vs lacunar infarcts — perivascular spaces are CSF density, round/linear, along perforators, no mass effect or surrounding gliosis.
- **High-riding jugular bulb** — recognise on bone windows, do not mistake for a lytic lesion.

---

## Pitfalls Worth Memorising

- **Pseudo-haemorrhage from calcification:** confirm with bone windows and HU measurement.
- **Hyperdense sinuses in dehydration/polycythaemia:** look for expansion and compare sides before calling thrombosis.
- **Partial volume at vertex and skull base:** adjacent bone falsely elevates HU in cortex or subdural space.
- **Symmetry trap:** diffuse hypoxic-ischaemic injury can look deceptively normal — assess cistern patency, sulcal calibre, and lentiform conspicuity.

---

## Clinical Correlations and When to Escalate

- **Midline shift:** <⚠️ [#38 REVIEW: ]<measure at the septum pellucidum at the level of the foramen of Monro; any shift is abnormal in the acute context.>> Use the pineal and third ventricle to detect subtle shift.
- **Cisternal effacement** raises concern for impending herniation — escalate.
- **Hydrocephalus:** early temporal horn dilatation, third ventricular bowing, and <⚠️ [#39 REVIEW: ]<Evans index >0.3>> warrant urgent neurosurgical discussion, especially with effaced sulci or transependymal oedema.
- **Atrophy vs hydrocephalus:** atrophy widens both sulci and ventricles with preserved cisterns; hydrocephalus shows disproportionate temporal horn and third ventricular enlargement, effaced sulci, and possibly transependymal low attenuation.

---

## Quick Check-list for "Normal" on Acute CT

- No fracture; sutures appropriate for age.
- Symmetric sulcal CSF; no crescentic or lentiform extra-axial collection.
- <⚠️ [#40 REVIEW: ]<Ventricles appropriate for age; temporal horns slit-like; third ventricle 2–6 mm; aqueduct and 4th ventricle midline and patent.>>
- Basal cisterns patent.
- <⚠️ [#41 REVIEW: ]<Grey > white by ~5–10 HU>>; insular ribbon and lentiform nuclei sharp.
- Septum, pineal, third ventricle midline.
- Only physiological vascular wall calcifications; sinuses normal calibre and not expanded.
- Globes intact; sinuses and mastoids aerated.

Mastering normal — including age-appropriate variants, benign calcifications, and the typical HU profiles — is what allows confident detection of acute pathology and avoidance of the common over- and under-calls.
