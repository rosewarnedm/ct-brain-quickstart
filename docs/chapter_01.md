# CT Physics and Technique for Acute Brain CT: A Practical Guide for ED Clinicians

---

## Why this matters in the ED

You do not need to know how a CT scanner is engineered, but you do need to understand what the numbers on the screen mean, why certain findings are easy to miss, and how the way a scan was acquired or displayed affects your interpretation. Getting this wrong can mean missing a bleed or misidentifying contrast staining as haemorrhage.

---

## Hounsfield Units (HU): the language of CT

Everything on a CT scan is expressed as a Hounsfield Unit (HU) value — a measure of how much the tissue attenuates the X-ray beam, calibrated against water (0 HU) and air (−1000 HU).

**Memorise these reference values:**

| Structure | Approximate HU |
|---|---|
| Air | −1000 |
| Fat | −30 to −100 |
| CSF | 0 to +15 |
| White matter | 20–30 |
| Grey matter | 30–40 |
| Acute clotted haemorrhage | 50–80 |
| Subacute haemorrhage | 30–40 |
| Chronic haemorrhage | 0–20 |
| Arterial thrombus | 55–75 |
| Calcification | 100–300+ |
| Iodinated contrast in vessels | 100–300 |

The grey–white matter difference is only **5–10 HU** — this is why early infarction, which causes a drop of just 2–4 HU, is so easy to miss. Conversely, a hyperdense focus above 150 HU is almost certainly calcification or dense iodine, not blood.

## What the numbers tell you clinically

### Acute haemorrhage
Fresh, clotted blood is hyperdense (**50–80 HU**) because of its high protein and packed red cell content. As a haematoma ages, attenuation falls:

- **Acute (up to ~7 days):** 60–80 HU — clearly hyperdense, sharp margins, often with a surrounding hypodense oedema rim
- **Subacute (~1–2 weeks):** 30–40 HU — **isodense to brain** — easily missed; look for mass effect and effaced sulci instead
- **Chronic (>2 weeks):** 0–20 HU — hypodense, approaching CSF density; may develop a membrane/capsule

> **Practical tip:** An isodense subdural haematoma is one of the most common serious CT misses. If sulci are effaced, there is midline shift, or the cortex appears inwardly displaced, scroll carefully through coronal reformats even if no obvious hyperdensity is present.

### Distinguishing hyperdense foci
When you see a bright (hyperdense) focus, ask:

- **Is it haemorrhage?** HU 50–80, mass effect, oedema rim, evolves over time
- **Is it calcification?** HU >150–200, sharp margins, no mass effect, typical sites (globus pallidus, choroid plexus, falx), unchanged on serial scans
- **Is it iodine contrast or staining?** HU often >100–150, vascular or grey–white junction distribution, fades on delayed imaging 12–24 hours later
- **Is it arterial thrombus (hyperdense vessel sign)?** Linear, tubular, HU ~55–75 — look at the MCA M1 segment and compare with the contralateral side

### Early infarction
Cytotoxic oedema reduces tissue attenuation by pulling water into cells. The first visible changes occur in the most metabolically active grey matter:

- **Insular ribbon sign:** loss of the sharp grey–white boundary at the insula
- **Lentiform obscuration:** the lentiform nucleus becomes indistinct
- **Sulcal effacement:** local swelling compresses overlying sulci
- **Hyperdense artery sign:** a clotted thrombus appears as a hyperdense MCA or basilar artery before any parenchymal change is visible

These early changes require **narrow window settings and thin slices** to detect. If you are assessing for ASPECTS in a stroke patient, do not review on default windows alone.

## Display settings: windows and levels

The single most important technical habit you can adopt is **toggling between window settings** before concluding a scan is normal.

| Setting | Window Level (WL) | Window Width (WW) | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brain (standard) | 35 | 80 | General parenchyma review |
| Blood/SAH | 50–60 | 110–150 | Subtle subarachnoid blood, thin subdurals |
| Posterior fossa | 35–40 | 160–200 | Reduces beam hardening artefact |
| Bone | 600 | 2800 | Fractures, skull base, calcification |

> **Key habit:** Always review every acute head CT on at least brain windows, blood windows, and bone windows. A scan that looks normal on standard brain windows may reveal subarachnoid blood on blood windows, or a skull base fracture on bone windows.

## Slice thickness and reconstructions

The standard **4–5 mm axial** slices sent to PACS for primary review are adequate for most findings, but important pathology can be concealed by **partial volume averaging** — where a thin structure (small subdural, thin subarachnoid blood, hairline fracture) is averaged with adjacent tissue and disappears.

**Whenever you are uncertain:**
- Request or review the **thin-slice series (0.5–1 mm)** — these should be available on PACS as a separate series
- Review **coronal and sagittal reformats (MPR)** — particularly valuable for:
  - Convexity and tentorial subdurals
  - Skull base fractures
  - Small extra-axial collections at the vertex

## Common artefacts you must recognise

### Beam hardening (posterior fossa)
Thick petrous bone causes streak artefacts and bands of artificially low attenuation in the posterior fossa. This can:
- Mimic or obscure a brainstem or cerebellar infarct
- Create pseudo-hyperdensity mimicking haemorrhage

**Mitigation:** Use the dedicated posterior fossa window setting (WW 160–200). Review thin slices. Maintain clinical suspicion regardless of CT appearances if symptoms localise to the posterior fossa — MRI is far superior here.

### Tentorial mimic of SAH
The tentorium is naturally dense and, on thick axial slices, can appear to fill the ambient and suprasellar cisterns, mimicking subarachnoid haemorrhage. Confirm on **thin slices in multiple planes** before diagnosing SAH in this location.

### Pseudo-SAH in diffuse cerebral oedema
In severe hypoxic–ischaemic injury (e.g. post-cardiac arrest), the brain parenchyma becomes globally hypodense, making normal vessels and the tentorium appear relatively hyperdense — simulating SAH. Clues:
- Diffusely low brain attenuation throughout
- No true sulcal layering of blood
- Clinical context (post-arrest, anoxic injury)
- Do not phone the neurosurgeons for an aneurysm coiling in this situation

### Motion artefact
Blurring and double contours — ask the clinical team whether the patient was moving. A limited repeat of a critical area may be warranted. Do not over-interpret subtle findings on a motion-degraded study.

### High haematocrit and polycythaemia
Dehydrated or polycythaemic patients have naturally higher blood attenuation. Vessels may appear mildly hyperdense bilaterally without thrombosis. Always compare to the contralateral side and use ratios rather than absolute numbers alone.

## Iodine versus haemorrhage after stroke treatment

This is a common and clinically important problem. After thrombolysis or thrombectomy, disrupted blood–brain barrier allows iodinated contrast to extravasate into brain tissue (contrast staining), which can look identical to haemorrhage on standard CT.

**How to approach this:**
- HU >100–150 after a procedure: strongly consider iodine staining, but overlap exists
- **Delayed NCCT at 12–24 hours:** iodine staining fades; true haemorrhage persists or increases
- **Dual-energy CT (DECT):** if available, produces an iodine map and a virtual non-contrast image — iodine staining appears on the iodine map but disappears on the virtual non-contrast; haemorrhage persists on both
- This distinction directly affects whether you can safely start or continue antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy — escalate to the stroke team or neuroradiologist if uncertain

## Dural venous sinus thrombosis (DVST)

- Acute thrombus in a dural sinus appears **hyperdense (~60–75 HU)** — the "dense sinus sign"
- A thrombosed cortical vein appears as a **curvilinear hyperdensity** — the "cord sign"
- Distinguish from contrast-opacified sinuses (check timing of imaging relative to injection) and from high haematocrit
- Confirm with **CT venography (CTV)** or MRI/MR venography — a filling defect (the "empty delta sign") in the enhancing sinus confirms the diagnosis

## Practical protocol: what to expect on an acute head CT

A standard acute NCCT should provide:
- **4–5 mm axial soft-tissue series** for primary review
- **Thin-slice series (~0.5–1 mm)** for problem-solving
- **Coronal and sagittal MPR** — request these if not automatically generated
- **Bone series** — always review this

For stroke workup, CTA of the head and neck is performed immediately after NCCT. CT perfusion (CTP) adds maps of cerebral blood flow (CBF), blood volume (CBV), and time-to-maximum (Tmax) to assess ischaemic core versus salvageable penumbra — interpretation should involve the stroke team and radiology.

---

## Contrast safety: the basics for the ED

- **Anaphylaxis to iodinated contrast** is uncommon but can be severe — resuscitation facilities must be available for CTA/CTP
- **Contrast-induced kidney injury** risk is low with modern agents, but exercise caution if **eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m²** in non-emergency situations; in a life-threatening emergency, proceed and manage the kidneys afterwards
- **Pregnancy:** fetal dose from head CT is negligible; shield the abdomen; clinical urgency almost always outweighs theoretical risk — discuss with the treating team and radiology

## "Don't miss" checklist for acute head CT

Before signing off any acute NCCT as normal, confirm you have:

- [ ] Reviewed on **brain, blood, and bone windows**
- [ ] Checked **posterior fossa on wider windows** (WW 160–200)
- [ ] Reviewed **thin slices and MPR** if any doubt about extra-axial collections, skull base, or posterior fossa
- [ ] Looked specifically at the **basal cisterns and sylvian fissures** for subarachnoid blood on blood windows
- [ ] Assessed **grey–white differentiation** at the insula, lentiform nucleus, and cortex on narrow windows for early infarction
- [ ] Checked for **hyperdense artery sign** (MCA, basilar)
- [ ] Reviewed **dural sinuses** for asymmetric hyperdensity (DVST)
- [ ] Considered whether a finding is isodense haematoma with **mass effect/sulcal effacement** rather than absent haemorrhage
- [ ] Measured **HU** of any suspicious hyperdense focus rather than relying on gestalt
- [ ] Escalated to radiology if you are uncertain — especially for posterior fossa pathology, equivocal SAH, or iodine versus haemorrhage post-intervention

## When to escalate immediately

- Any suspected or confirmed **intracranial haemorrhage** — neurosurgery/stroke team
- **Hyperdense artery sign** or early ischaemic change — stroke team for thrombectomy pathway
- **Equivocal SAH** — discuss with radiology; consider LP if safe, or CTA to look for an aneurysm
- **Posterior fossa findings** — low CT sensitivity; if symptoms localise here and CT looks normal, escalate for urgent MRI
- **Possible DVST** — CTV or MRI urgently
- **Iodine versus haemorrhage** post-reperfusion therapy — discuss antithrombotic management with stroke team; arrange DECT or delayed CT
