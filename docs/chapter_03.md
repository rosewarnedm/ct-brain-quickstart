# Chapter 3: A Systematic Approach to Reading Acute Brain CT

## Why CT First?
Non-contrast CT (NCCT) is the front-line triage tool in acute neurology. It is fast, accessible, and reliably detects acute haemorrhage, mass effect, hydrocephalus, and fractures. Its three core jobs in the ED are:
- **Exclude haemorrhage before thrombolysis.**
- **Identify haemorrhage requiring neurosurgical input.**
- **Detect features that predict benefit from thrombectomy.**

## Hounsfield Units You Must Know
Attenuation values anchor pattern recognition. Approximate normal values:

- <⚠️ [#42 REVIEW: ]<Water/CSF: 0 to +15 HU>>
- <⚠️ [#43 REVIEW: ]<Normal white matter ~20–30 HU; normal grey matter ~35–45 HU (grey–white difference ~5–10 HU)>>
- <⚠️ [#44 REVIEW: ]<Acute clotted blood: ~60–80 HU>>
- <⚠️ [#45 REVIEW: ]<Calcification: usually >80–100 HU (often 150–300 HU)>>
- Cortical bone: >1000 HU

Always use ROIs and side-to-side comparisons; HU vary with scanner, kernel, and beam hardening.

## Acquisition and Display
Verify patient, contrast status, and coverage (foramen magnum to vertex). Use multiple windows:
- <⚠️ [#46 REVIEW: ]<Brain: W 80, L 40>>
- <⚠️ [#47 REVIEW: ]<Subarachnoid/blood window: W 200, L 80 — increases conspicuity of basal SAH>>
- <⚠️ [#48 REVIEW: ]<Bone: W 2500, L 500>>

Always review MPRs — coronal for skull base/vertex, sagittal for craniocervical junction. Beware streak/beam-hardening at the skull base, which mimics or hides haemorrhage.

## A Reading Sequence
Read outside-in, top-to-toe:
1. Scalp, orbits, facial/temporal bones
2. Calvarium and skull base
3. Extra-axial spaces (EDH, SDH, SAH)
4. Ventricles and cisterns
5. Parenchyma (grey–white, deep nuclei, brainstem, cerebellum)
6. Mass effect and herniation
7. Vascular clues
8. Sinuses/mastoids
9. Pitfalls/review areas
10. Synthesis and management-facing impression

## 1) Scalp, Orbits, Temporal Bones
- **Scalp haematoma** marks the impact site — search for fracture beneath.
- **Retrobulbar haematoma**: look for globe tenting and orbital apex stranding — **emergent ophthalmology referral if compartment syndrome suspected**.
- **Temporal bone**: haemotympanum, ossicular disruption, carotid canal fracture (ICA injury risk).

## 2) Calvarium and Skull Base
Distinguish fractures from sutures and vascular grooves (sutures have corticated edges). Basilar fracture clues: air in Meckel's cave/cavernous sinus, mastoid effusion, sphenoid fluid level, carotid canal involvement. **Depressed fractures greater than skull thickness, compound wounds, or carotid canal involvement warrant neurosurgical assessment ± CTA.**

## 3) Extra-axial Haemorrhage

### Epidural Haematoma (EDH)
- **Biconvex/lentiform hyperdensity, <⚠️ [#49 REVIEW: ]<limited by sutures but can cross dural reflections>>**, often with overlying skull fracture.
- "**Swirl sign**" (hypodense areas within hyperdense clot) = active bleeding.
- Volume by **<⚠️ [#50 REVIEW: ]<ABC/2>>**.
- <⚠️ [#51 REVIEW: ]<Surgical thresholds: thickness >15 mm, volume >30 mL, midline shift >5 mm, or neurological decline.>>

### Subdural Haematoma (SDH)
- Crescentic; <⚠️ [#52 REVIEW: ]<crosses sutures but limited by dural reflections (falx, tentorium)>>.
- Density evolution:
  - <⚠️ [#53 REVIEW: ]<Acute (0–3 d): hyperdense 60–80 HU>>
  - <⚠️ [#54 REVIEW: ]<Early subacute (3–14 d): isodense to brain (~30–40 HU) — highest miss risk>>
  - <⚠️ [#55 REVIEW: ]<Chronic (>2–3 weeks): hypodense (~0–20 HU)>>
- <⚠️ [#56 REVIEW: ]<Surgical evacuation considered for acute SDH thickness ≥10 mm or midline shift ≥5 mm.>>
- **For isodense subacute SDH**: look for asymmetric sulcal effacement, inward displacement of the cortex from the inner table, and inwardly-displaced cortical veins. Bilateral isodense SDHs can mimic atrophy.

### Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)
- Hyperdensity (50–70 HU) in basal cisterns, Sylvian fissures, sulci. Frequent intraventricular extension.
- <⚠️ [#57 REVIEW: ]<NCCT sensitivity is highest within 6 hours (>95%), declining thereafter.>>
- **Perimesencephalic-only pattern** suggests non-aneurysmal venous bleed; asymmetric Sylvian/cisternal blood points toward the aneurysm side.
- **Treat aneurysmal SAH as a time-critical emergency**: immediate CTA; if CT is negative late after onset with high suspicion, follow local LP/MR pathway.
- **Pseudo-SAH pitfall**: in diffuse cerebral oedema, low brain attenuation makes engorged cisterns look bright. <⚠️ [#58 REVIEW: ]<True SAH is ~50–70 HU; pseudo-SAH cisternal density is typically <40–45 HU.>> Recent contrast or high haematocrit can also raise cisternal density.

## 4) Ventricles and Cisterns
- **Obstructive hydrocephalus**: disproportionate upstream ventricular enlargement with **transependymal CSF permeation** (periventricular hypoattenuation). Early sign: **temporal horn dilatation** and third ventricle bowing.
- **Communicating hydrocephalus**: global ventricular enlargement.
- <⚠️ [#59 REVIEW: ]<Evans' index (frontal horn width / biparietal diameter) >0.3 suggests ventriculomegaly.>>
- **Acute obstructive hydrocephalus with clinical decline → urgent EVD.**
- Differentiate atrophy (sulci AND ventricles enlarge) from hydrocephalus (ventricles enlarge with sulcal effacement and transependymal change).

## 5) Parenchyma — Early Ischaemia and Oedema

### Early ischaemic signs (minutes–hours)
- Loss of insular ribbon
- Obscuration of the lentiform nucleus
- Cortical hypoattenuation and sulcal effacement
- Hyperdense artery sign

### ASPECTS (MCA territory)
- <⚠️ [#60 REVIEW: ]<10-point score; subtract 1 for each affected region.>>
- <⚠️ [#61 REVIEW: ]<ASPECTS ≥6 has historically favoured IVT/thrombectomy; lower scores (3–5) may still be eligible based on advanced imaging mismatch — follow local protocol.>>

### Mimics of infarct
- **Post-ictal swelling**: gyriform low attenuation that does not respect a vascular territory; often hippocampal.
- **Hypoglycaemia/HIE**: bilateral, symmetric cortical and deep grey involvement; in severe oedema may give pseudo-SAH sign.

### Oedema patterns
- **Vasogenic** (white matter, finger-like): tumour, abscess, PRES.
- **Cytotoxic** (grey + white, vascular territory): acute infarct.
- **Interstitial** (periventricular): hydrocephalus.

## 6) Mass Effect and Herniation
- **Measure midline shift in mm at the septum pellucidum.**
- Watch the cisterns — **suprasellar, ambient, quadrigeminal, perimesencephalic effacement signals herniation risk**.
- Patterns:
  - **Subfalcine** → cingulate under falx; risk of ACA infarct.
  - **Uncal** → ipsilateral PCA infarct, effaced ipsilateral ambient cistern, midbrain compression.
  - **Central downward** → effaced suprasellar cistern, small ventricles.
  - **Tonsillar** → foramen magnum crowding (use sagittal MPR).
  - **Upward transtentorial** (posterior fossa mass) → effaced quadrigeminal cistern.
- Herniation mandates urgent escalation: decompression, EVD, osmotherapy, BP control.

## 7) Parenchymal and Intraventricular Haemorrhage

### Intracerebral haemorrhage (ICH)
- <⚠️ [#62 REVIEW: ]<Acute: 60–80 HU>>; round/ovoid hyperdensity.
- **Hypertensive locations**: putamen, thalamus, pons, cerebellum.
- **Lobar in elderly** → consider cerebral amyloid angiopathy, not just hypertension.
- **Volume by <⚠️ [#63 REVIEW: ]<ABC/2>>**; large volume = poor prognosis.
- **Expansion markers** (suggest active bleeding): blend sign, black hole sign, irregular margins, fluid–blood levels.
- <⚠️ [#64 REVIEW: ]<Cerebellar ICH >3 cm, brainstem compression, or hydrocephalus → surgical consideration.>>
- Always: BP control, reverse coagulopathy.

### Intraventricular haemorrhage (IVH)
- Layering hyperdensity in dependent occipital horns/trigones.
- **Watch for casted fourth ventricle and obstructive hydrocephalus.**
- Distinguish from choroid plexus/pineal calcifications: <⚠️ [#65 REVIEW: ]<calcifications are 150–300 HU with sharply corticated margins>>.

## 8) Vascular Clues on NCCT

### Hyperdense artery sign
- <⚠️ [#66 REVIEW: ]<Intraluminal MCA thrombus ~60–70 HU vs adjacent brain ~35–45 HU.>> Always compare to the contralateral MCA and venous sinuses.
- **Dot sign** = M2/insular branch occlusion.
- **Hyperdense basilar** in a comatose patient — call immediately.
- **Differentiate from calcification**: calcium is denser, curvilinear, and circumferential. Thrombus conforms to the lumen.
- Globally dense vessels in dehydration/polycythaemia — use side-to-side and artery:brain ratio.

### Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (CVST)
- <⚠️ [#67 REVIEW: ]<Hyperdense sinus >60–70 HU, asymmetric vs contralateral side.>>
- **Cord sign**: hyperdense cortical vein.
- High haematocrit raises sinus density physiologically — <⚠️ [#68 REVIEW: ]<HU/haematocrit ratio >1.3–1.5 suggests thrombosis (interpret with caution).>>
- Hypoplastic transverse sinus is a common mimic — check the bony groove.

## 9) Infection and Inflammation
- **Meningitis**: NCCT often normal; subtle sulcal effacement, hyperdense cisternal exudate, ventriculitis, hydrocephalus.
- **Abscess**: low-attenuation centre with disproportionate vasogenic oedema.
- **HSV encephalitis**: low attenuation in **medial temporal lobes, insula, cingulate** — non-vascular distribution. **Start aciclovir empirically; arrange MRI.**

## 10) Tumour-Related Pitfalls
- **Haemorrhagic tumour** = lobar bleed with disproportionate vasogenic oedema, irregular margins, multifocality. Classic bleeding metastases: **melanoma, RCC, choriocarcinoma, thyroid**.
- **Lymphoma**: often hyperdense to cortex on NCCT, homogeneous.
- Don't reflexively label every lobar bleed "hypertensive" — consider amyloid (elderly, cortical/subcortical, multifocal).
