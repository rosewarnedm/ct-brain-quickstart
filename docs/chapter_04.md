# Extradural Haematoma — ED & Junior Radiologist Summary

---

## Overview

Extradural haematoma (EDH) is a neurosurgical emergency. Blood accumulates between the inner skull table and the periosteal dura. The classic cause is arterial injury — usually the middle meningeal artery (MMA) at the pterion — following a skull fracture. Venous sources (dural sinuses, diploic veins) also occur, particularly at the vertex and posterior fossa. Early recognition on CT directly changes outcome.

---

## Pathophysiology and Mechanism of Injury

The dura is tightly adherent at suture lines and venous sinus attachments. This anatomy controls where EDH spreads and what it looks like:

- <mark>EDH does not cross suture lines</mark> — this is its defining anatomical constraint.
- Subdural haematoma (SDH) crosses sutures freely but is limited by dural reflections (falx, tentorium) — the opposite pattern.
- Arterial EDH (MMA territory) expands rapidly under arterial pressure. Venous EDH expands more slowly but is equally dangerous in confined spaces such as the posterior fossa.
- Children and young adults are more susceptible because the dura is less adherent to the skull than in the elderly.
- The classic "lucid interval" (transient improvement followed by rapid deterioration) reflects compensated then decompensated raised intracranial pressure. It is not universal — do not use its absence to exclude EDH.

---

## Clinical Context and Management Correlates

Specifically search for EDH when there is:
- Focal scalp swelling or impact site, especially temporoparietal.
- A visible skull fracture on bone windows.
- Rapid neurological deterioration, unequal pupils, or lateralising deficit.

**Operative thresholds commonly applied (communicate these explicitly):**
- <mark>Thickness >15 mm or volume >30 mL, midline shift >5 mm, focal neurological deficit, or GCS <9</mark> — these are commonly cited indications for surgical evacuation.
- Posterior fossa EDH carries a lower volumetric threshold for intervention — even small collections compress the brainstem and cause acute obstructive hydrocephalus.

**Conservative management criteria (institutional protocols vary):**
- <mark>Thickness <15 mm, volume <30 mL, no focal deficit, minimal midline shift</mark> — may be observed, but requires <mark>repeat CT at 6–8 hours</mark> to detect early expansion.

---

## CT Technique and Practical Approach

- Non-contrast CT head with <mark>thin slices (≤1–2 mm)</mark>, reconstructed in axial, coronal, and sagittal planes.
- Always review brain windows AND bone windows.
- Use a widened "subdural" window (<mark>W 250–300, L 50</mark>) to improve detection of subtle extra-axial collections.
- Add CTA if active bleeding or venous sinus injury is suspected (vertex, posterior fossa, or contrast blush on non-contrast CT).

**Systematic checklist:**
1. Scalp swelling and impact site.
2. Skull fractures and sutural diastasis on bone windows.
3. Extra-axial collections: density, shape, relation to sutures, internal heterogeneity.
4. Mass effect: sulcal effacement, ventricular compression, midline shift, cisternal effacement.
5. Herniation signs: uncal, central, tonsillar.
6. Associated injuries: contusions, subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH), SDH.

---

## Non-Contrast CT Findings in Detail

### Density (Hounsfield Units) and Internal Architecture

- <mark>Acute clotted blood: typically 60–80 HU</mark> — appears hyperdense relative to brain.
- <mark>The "swirl sign" — regions of relative hypoattenuation (30–45 HU) within a hyperdense haematoma</mark> — indicates active, ongoing haemorrhage. This is a critical finding signalling haemodynamic instability and high risk of rapid expansion.
- In anaemia, haemorrhage attenuation falls. <mark>Acute EDH may appear mildly hyperdense or even isodense (~30–40 HU) in a severely anaemic patient</mark> — adjust windowing, use coronal/sagittal reformats, and look for mass effect clues.

### Morphology and Distribution

- **Classic shape:** lentiform (biconvex), with a convex inner margin pushing into the brain and sharp outer margin conforming to the skull.
- Tapering ends typically abut suture lines.
- **Most common location:** temporoparietal (MMA territory). Also frontal, parietal, occipital; vertex (venous); posterior fossa.
- Usually solitary and ipsilateral to impact. Bilateral EDH can occur with high-energy vertex injuries.

### Associated Osseous Findings

- <mark>Skull fracture is present in approximately 80–95% of EDH</mark> — its absence does not exclude EDH, but its presence should always trigger a focused search for underlying haemorrhage.
- A linear fracture crossing the MMA groove is the classic finding. Also look for sutural diastasis (children) and depressed fractures.
- Bone windows are essential — do not skip them.

### Mass Effect and Secondary Signs

- Sulcal effacement, ipsilateral ventricular compression, and midline shift are key markers of significant mass effect.
- <mark>Midline shift is measured at the septum pellucidum</mark>.
- Uncal herniation: effacement of the ipsilateral ambient cistern, medial temporal lobe displacement, midbrain compression.
- Posterior fossa EDH: fourth ventricular compression, aqueductal obstruction, acute hydrocephalus, and tonsillar or upward transtentorial herniation.
- Duret haemorrhages (brainstem) indicate severe downward herniation and carry a poor prognosis.

### Evolution Over Time

| Phase | Timing | Approximate HU |
|---|---|---|
| Early acute | 0–24 h | <mark>60–80 HU</mark> (uniform) or mixed (swirl) |
| Late acute/early subacute | 1–7 days | <mark>40–60 HU</mark> |
| Subacute | 1–3 weeks | <mark>30–40 HU</mark> (approaching isodensity) |
| Chronic | >3 weeks | <mark>10–20 HU</mark> (hypodense) |

Interval enlargement within the <mark>first 6–24 hours</mark> is common — early repeat CT is justified in conservatively managed patients.

---

## Special Locations and Variants

### Vertex (Parasagittal) EDH
- Usually venous (superior sagittal sinus or diploic veins).
- **Easily missed on axial-only review** due to partial volume averaging at the vertex.
- <mark>Coronal and sagittal reconstructions are essential to detect vertex EDH</mark>.
- May appear flattened or irregular rather than classically biconvex.

### Posterior Fossa EDH
- Related to transverse/sigmoid sinus or occipital arterial injury.
- <mark>Even small posterior fossa EDH can be catastrophic</mark> — the tight compartment leaves little reserve.
- Always assess fourth ventricle compression and look for early obstructive hydrocephalus.

### Interhemispheric EDH
- Rare; arises from anterior meningeal artery with parasagittal fracture.
- Triangular/irregular hyperdensity abutting the falx.
- Do not misclassify as interhemispheric SDH — use anatomical constraints and contour to differentiate.

---

## Contrast-Enhanced CT and CTA

Not required for diagnosis in most cases. Add CTA when:
- **Contrast blush** is suspected within the EDH on non-contrast CT (predicts rapid expansion).
- Dural venous sinus injury is a concern (vertex, posterior fossa) — CT venography delineates sinus laceration or thrombosis.
- MMA pseudoaneurysm or complex vascular injury is considered.

---

## Measurement and Reporting Essentials

**Measure and report:**
- Maximal thickness (mm).
- <mark>Volume using ABC/2</mark> (A = maximal length; B = maximal orthogonal width; C = number of slices × slice thickness) — an ellipsoid approximation, pragmatic and widely used.
- <mark>Midline shift at the septum pellucidum (mm)</mark>.

**Always describe:**
- Location with bony landmarks, laterality, relation to sutures.
- Shape (classic vs atypical), internal heterogeneity (swirl sign).
- Associated skull fracture and implicated vascular territory.
- Mass effect, herniation, associated injuries (contusion, SAH, SDH).
- Whether measurements approach or meet surgical thresholds.
- Interval change if prior imaging is available.

---

## Key Differential Diagnoses and Distinguishing Features

| Feature | EDH | Acute SDH |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | Lentiform (biconvex) | Crescentic (concave inner margin) |
| Crosses sutures? | No | Yes |
| Limited by dural reflections? | No | Yes (falx, tentorium) |
| Cortical veins | Displaced away from skull | May be seen tracking along inner table |
| Mechanism | Arterial/venous; focal impact | Bridging veins; often diffuse injury |

**Other differentials:**
- **Extradural empyema/abscess:** lower attenuation (~20–40 HU), may contain gas, rim enhancement on contrast CT, restricted diffusion on MRI, clinical sepsis — no acute fracture.
- **Subgaleal/cephalohaematoma:** located *outside* the calvarium — bone windows confirm the intact inner table; do not confuse with intracranial EDH.
- **Hyperdense meningioma:** well-circumscribed, ± calcification, dural tail, bone hyperostosis — no acute trauma history, does not conform as an acute biconvex haemorrhage.
- **Epidural fat/air:** fat is <mark>−50 to −100 HU</mark>; air is <mark>−800 to −1000 HU</mark> — distinguishable by density alone.

---

## Imaging Pitfalls and Common Misses

- **Vertex EDH missed on axial-only review** — always use coronal and sagittal reformats; widen window width.
- **Isoattenuating EDH** (anaemia, hyperacute phase) — compare both sides, look for lentiform contour and subtle mass effect, adjust windowing.
- **EDH vs SDH misclassification** — use the suture rule, inner margin contour, and cortical vein position.
- **Scalp vs intracranial collection confusion** — bone windows confirm whether the collection is inside or outside the inner table.
- **Posterior fossa EDH underestimation** — the tight compartment means small volumes cause critical effects; explicitly assess the fourth ventricle and cisterna magna.
- **Missed skull base/temporal bone fractures** — review bone windows carefully, including the carotid canal, otic capsule, and venous sinus plates.
- **Swirl sign under-called** — recognise mixed density as a marker of active haemorrhage and escalate urgently.

---

## Practical Pearls

- **The CT triad:** lens-shaped collection + suture-limited + overlying a fracture.
- Always state the fracture, the implicated vessel (e.g., "linear temporal fracture crossing the MMA groove"), and whether surgical thresholds are met.
- <mark>In the posterior fossa, even a small EDH requires urgent neurosurgical communication.</mark>
- Quantify: thickness, volume (ABC/2), midline shift, cisternal status, and active bleeding signs.
- If managing conservatively, document that <mark>repeat CT at 6–8 hours</mark> is planned.

**Q5.** You are reviewing the CT of a 67-year-old woman who takes warfarin and sustained a ground-level fall. There is a right-sided extra-axial collection. Within the predominantly hyperdense collection, you notice irregular areas of markedly lower attenuation measuring approximately 35 HU.

What does this internal heterogeneity most likely represent, and what is the key management implication?

A. Subacute blood products indicating the collection is several weeks old; reassurance appropriate  
B. Mixed acute and chronic haemorrhage from a pre-existing collection; standard observation  
C. Active ongoing haemorrhage ("swirl sign"); high risk of rapid expansion requiring urgent escalation  
D. Liquefied clot consistent with an extradural abscess; commence antibiotics  
E. Artefact from beam hardening near the skull; no action required

---

### Answers

**Q1. C** — <mark>Thickness >15 mm and midline shift >5 mm</mark> are commonly cited surgical thresholds; this collection (16 mm thick, 6 mm shift) meets both, combined with clinical deterioration and anisocoria, mandating immediate neurosurgical referral.

- **A** is wrong: a skull fracture alone is not an operative indication; it prompts targeted search but does not mandate surgery in isolation.
- **B** is wrong: lentiform shape is a diagnostic descriptor indicating EDH rather than SDH, but does not itself determine surgical need.
- **D** is wrong: temporoparietal location identifies the vascular territory but is not itself a threshold for surgery.
- **E** is wrong: the lucid interval is a clinical observation of prognostic interest, not a CT or surgical threshold criterion.

---

**Q2. B** — <mark>In severe anaemia, haemoglobin-dependent attenuation falls, and acute EDH may appear isodense or only mildly hyperdense (~30–40 HU)</mark>; sulcal effacement and ventricular compression are the clues to the underlying collection.

- **A** is wrong: subacute collections (1–3 weeks) become isodense, but the clinical context is acute trauma in a young patient; subacute evolution would require a different timeline.
- **C** is wrong: a CSF collection would be hypodense (~0–10 HU), not isodense to grey matter, and would not typically cause this degree of mass effect acutely.
- **D** is wrong: window width affects perceived contrast but does not change the absolute Hounsfield unit values of the collection itself.
- **E** is wrong: extradural empyema typically appears hypodense (~20–40 HU) with rim enhancement and gas, in a septic patient — not an acute trauma presentation.

---

**Q3. C** — Vertex EDH is easily missed on axial CT because partial volume averaging at the superior calvarium obscures the collection; coronal and sagittal reconstructions are essential for detection at this location.

- **A** is wrong: the collection was identified on reformatted images, confirming it is detectable by CT.
- **B** is wrong: acute blood is hyperdense (60–80 HU); the issue is geometric, not density-related.
- **D** is wrong: vertex EDH is not invariably isodense and does not routinely require MRI for diagnosis when adequate CT reformats are obtained.
- **E** is wrong: standard head CT protocols include the vertex; field-of-view limitation is not the explanation here.

---

**Q4. C** — <mark>Posterior fossa EDH carries a lower volumetric threshold for surgical intervention</mark> because the tight compartment means even modest collections cause brainstem compression and acute obstructive hydrocephalus; fourth ventricular compression is already present, mandating immediate neurosurgical referral.

- **A** is wrong: the 30 mL threshold applies to supratentorial EDH; it cannot be applied to the posterior fossa, where even smaller volumes are dangerous.
- **B** is wrong: a 48-hour observation interval is inappropriate when there is already fourth ventricular compression on the initial scan.
- **D** is wrong: MRI is not necessary when CT findings are diagnostic and the clinical situation requires urgent action; delay could be harmful.
- **E** is wrong: tranexamic acid may have a role in haemorrhage but does not address established mass effect on the fourth ventricle and is not a substitute for neurosurgical referral.

---

**Q5. C** — <mark>The "swirl sign" — areas of relative hypoattenuation (30–45 HU) within a hyperdense haematoma — indicates active, ongoing haemorrhage</mark> and is a marker of haemodynamic instability and high risk of rapid expansion, requiring urgent escalation.

- **A** is wrong: subacute collections show uniform reduction in attenuation over days to weeks, not focal irregular hypoattenuating swirls within acute hyperdense blood in an acute trauma patient.
- **B** is wrong: layering of acute on chronic blood can produce mixed density but the swirl sign specifically indicates active arterial extravasation, not simply blood of different ages.
- **D** is wrong: an abscess would be hypodense throughout, not hyperdense with internal swirling, and the clinical context is acute trauma, not infection.
- **E** is wrong: beam hardening artefacts are typically linear and symmetrical; irregular internal hypoattenuation within a hyperdense collection in the correct clinical context should not be dismissed as artefact.
