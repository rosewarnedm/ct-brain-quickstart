# Extradural Haematoma: ED and Junior Radiologist Summary

---

## Overview

Extradural haematoma (EDH) is a collection of blood between the inner table of the skull and the periosteal (outer) layer of the dura. It is a time-critical diagnosis: arterial sources drive rapid expansion and herniation. The classical CT appearance is a <mark>lentiform (biconvex) hyperdense extra-axial collection</mark> that is sharply marginated, limited by suture lines, and frequently adjacent to a skull fracture. Your job is to find it, characterise it, quantify it, and escalate promptly.

---

## Relevant Anatomy and Pathophysiology

The periosteal dura is tightly bound to the inner table at cranial sutures. This creates a natural barrier that molds the EDH into its biconvex shape and prevents it from crossing suture lines — this is your key differentiator from subdural haematoma (SDH), which lies deeper (between meningeal dura and arachnoid) and can cross sutures freely.

**Arterial sources** (most common): the middle meningeal artery (MMA) or its branches, typically torn by a temporal or parietal linear fracture crossing the MMA groove. Arterial pressure drives rapid, dangerous expansion.

**Venous sources**: dural venous sinuses (superior sagittal sinus [SSS], transverse/sigmoid) or diploic veins. More common in children. Expansion is slower but still life-threatening — particularly in the posterior fossa, where even small volumes compress the brainstem.

**Paediatric note**: A pliable skull and diastatic sutures mean EDH can occur without an obvious fracture. Bilateral parasagittal or vertex EDH suggests SSS injury.

---

## Mechanisms of Injury

- Temporal blow → linear fracture crossing MMA groove → arterial EDH (most common pattern)
- Occipital blow → posterior fossa EDH with sinus or occipital diastasis injury
- Parasagittal trauma → vertex EDH from SSS laceration
- Anticoagulation or coagulopathy → heterogeneous density, fluid–fluid levels, higher expansion risk

---

## CT Technique and General Reporting Approach

**Acquisition**: Non-contrast CT, <mark>thin slices (≤1–1.25 mm)</mark> with multiplanar reformats. Coronal reconstructions are essential for vertex and posterior fossa EDH. Always review bone windows for fractures and MMA groove disruption.

**Escalation prompts for CTA/CTV**:
- Unexplained expansion → CTA to detect active arterial extravasation or MMA pseudoaneurysm
- EDH abutting a venous sinus → CTV to assess for sinus laceration or thrombosis

**What to document in every report**:
- Side, location, compartment
- Maximal thickness (mm), dimensions (A×B×C), estimated volume <mark>(ABC/2 for an ellipsoid collection)</mark>
- HU density, homogeneity, swirl sign
- Associated injuries: fracture, contusions, SAH, SDH, DAI
- Mass effect: sulcal effacement, ventricular compression, midline shift (mm), cisternal effacement, herniation (uncal, subfalcine, tonsillar)
- Sinus involvement

---

## Non-contrast CT Findings

### Attenuation (Hounsfield Units) and Timing

| Phase | Timeframe | Approximate HU |
|---|---|---|
| Hyperacute (unclotted) | First minutes–hours | <mark>~30–45 HU</mark> |
| Acute (clotted) | Hours to ~3 days | <mark>~50–80 HU</mark> |
| Early subacute | Several days to ~2 weeks | <mark>~30–50 HU</mark> (declining) |
| Chronic | >>2–3 weeks | <mark>~10–30 HU</mark> (hypodense) |

**Critical caveat**: Very early EDH may be near-isodense with grey matter. If you see a fracture with subtle extra-axial convexity and mass effect, do not dismiss it — rescan if the patient deteriorates.

**Swirl sign**: Areas of relatively lower attenuation within an otherwise hyperdense collection. This represents active bleeding (unclotted blood mixing with clot). <mark>The swirl sign predicts ongoing expansion</mark> and requires urgent communication with the clinical team.

**Fluid–fluid levels**: Uncommon; when present, suspect coagulopathy, anticoagulation, or rebleeding.

### Morphology and Location

- **Shape**: Lentiform (biconvex), smooth inner margin, closely apposed outer margin to inner table
- **Suture limitation**: <mark>EDH does not cross suture lines</mark> — stops sharply at coronal, sagittal, or lambdoid sutures
- **Common sites**:
  - Temporal/parietal convexity (MMA-related, most common)
  - Vertex (SSS-related, may be bilateral parasagittal)
  - Posterior fossa (sinus-related, often occipital)

### Associated Findings

- Linear skull fracture adjacent to EDH in most adults (check bone windows)
- Fracture crossing the MMA groove is a high-yield finding
- Cortical contusions, SAH, or DAI beneath the fracture — these affect prognosis independently of EDH size

### Mass Effect and Herniation Risk

- **Convexity/temporal EDH**: Sulcal effacement → ventricular compression → midline shift → uncal herniation
- **Posterior fossa EDH**: 4th ventricle compression → obstructive hydrocephalus → tonsillar herniation. <mark>Small volumes in the posterior fossa can be life-threatening due to the limited compartment volume</mark>
- **Vertex EDH**: May compress the SSS → venous hypertension; look for engorged cortical veins

---

## Evolution Over Time

- First hours: Near-isodense — easily missed; shape and mass effect are your clues
- 6–72 hours: Uniformly hyperdense <mark>(50–80 HU)</mark>; swirl sign may develop
- Days to ~2 weeks: Density declines towards isodensity with brain; shape and sharp extra-axial margins persist as useful clues
- >2–3 weeks: Hypodense <mark>(10–30 HU)</mark>; gradually resorbs. Chronic EDH membranes or calcification are uncommon (unlike SDH)

Density falls approximately <mark>1–2 HU per day</mark> after the acute phase, but this is variable.

---

## Contrast-Enhanced CT, CTA, and CTV

- Routine contrast is **not needed** for EDH diagnosis
- **CTA**: Use when active arterial bleeding, MMA pseudoaneurysm, or unexplained expansion is suspected; also if endovascular treatment is being considered
- **CTV**: Use when EDH abuts or straddles the SSS, transverse, or sigmoid sinus — assess for laceration, compression, or thrombosis
- EDH itself **does not enhance**. Peripheral thin dural enhancement can be reactive. Thick nodular enhancement suggests an alternative diagnosis (meningioma, dural metastasis, empyema)

---

## Differential Diagnosis and Distinguishing Features

| Feature | EDH | Acute SDH |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | Lentiform/biconvex | Crescentic |
| Suture crossing | No — stops sharply | Yes — extends freely |
| Dural reflection limiting | No | Yes (falx, tentorium) |
| Cortical vein sign | Absent | May be present (veins visible within crescent) |
| Fracture association | Frequent | Less common |

**Subgaleal/cephalhaematoma**: Collection is *outside* the skull (superficial to the calvarial cortex). No intracranial mass effect. Confirm on bone and soft-tissue windows.

**Dural-based mass (meningioma/metastasis)**: Typically calcified, causes hyperostosis, enhances vividly, not trauma-related.

**Extradural empyema**: Lower density, possible gas, thick rim enhancement, associated sinusitis or osteomyelitis, clinical sepsis.

**Intra-diploic haematoma**: Within the diploic space; expands both skull tables — bone windows clarify the intraosseous location.

---

## Pitfalls and Common Misses

- **Very early EDH is near-isodense**: Trust the fracture and the shape. Request urgent rescan if any clinical deterioration
- **Vertex EDH**: Prone to beam-hardening and partial volume artefact at the skull vault. Always use thin slices and coronal reformats. Look for parasagittal lentiform collections limited by the sagittal suture
- **Posterior fossa EDH**: Complex bone makes it subtle on axials. Scrutinise the occipital inner table on both bone and soft-tissue windows. Small volumes carry major clinical impact
- **Misclassifying EDH as SDH**: Follow the collection along suture lines on bone windows. EDH stops; SDH continues. Identify the cortical vein sign to confirm SDH
- **Confusing extracranial collections with EDH**: Confirm the collection lies deep to the inner table using bone windows and multiplanar views
- **Under-calling swirl sign**: Mention it explicitly; it signals active haemorrhage and likely expansion
- **Missing venous sinus involvement**: Suspect it when EDH lies adjacent to a sinus. Unexplained raised ICP, sinus non-opacification, or engorged cortical veins are prompts for CTV
- **Subacute/chronic evolving EDH losing conspicuity**: HU falls towards isodensity. Rely on shape, sharp extra-axial interface, and suture limitation

---

## Clinical and Management Correlations

**EDH is a neurosurgical emergency until proven otherwise.**

**Typical adult thresholds for urgent surgical intervention** (protocols vary by institution; always discuss with neurosurgery):
- <mark>Volume >30 mL</mark>
- <mark>Maximal thickness >15 mm</mark>
- <mark>Midline shift >5 mm</mark>
- Any GCS decline or new focal neurological deficit

**Posterior fossa EDH**: <mark>Any posterior fossa EDH with neurological signs, or thickness >10–12 mm, warrants urgent neurosurgical discussion</mark> regardless of volume.

**Conservative management criteria**: <mark>Small EDH (<15 mm thick)</mark>, neurologically intact, minimal mass effect — may be observed with close monitoring and early repeat CT <mark>(e.g. at 6–12 hours)</mark>.

**Anticoagulated patients**: Higher expansion risk; expect heterogeneous density and possible fluid–fluid levels. Recommend early repeat CT. Reversal of anticoagulation should be considered as soon as it is safe.

**Paediatric patients**: Lower thresholds for admission and repeat imaging. Higher proportion of venous-source EDH; watch for delayed EDH after initial injury.

**"Lucid interval"**: Classical but occurs in a minority of patients. Its absence does not exclude EDH — always correlate mechanism, examination findings, and imaging.

**Vascular repair**: Active MMA extravasation or traumatic pseudoaneurysm may be managed endovascularly in selected cases.

## Key Take-Home Points

- <mark>EDH is classically lentiform, sharply marginated, and does not cross suture lines</mark>; acute density is <mark>50–80 HU</mark>
- Look hardest at the **vertex** and **posterior fossa** — thin slices and coronal reformats are non-negotiable
- <mark>The swirl sign signals active bleeding and likely expansion — communicate urgently</mark>
- Report all associated injuries: fracture, contusions, SAH, and venous sinus involvement
- <mark>Posterior fossa and temporal EDHs carry high herniation risk even at small volumes</mark> — always discuss with neurosurgery early

## Self-assessment MCQs

**Q1.** A 34-year-old man is brought to the ED after being struck on the right temple during an assault. He was briefly confused at the scene but walked to the ambulance unaided. On arrival his GCS is 15. Non-contrast CT shows a right temporal extra-axial collection with an estimated volume of 38 mL, maximal thickness of 18 mm, midline shift of 6 mm, and patchy areas of lower attenuation within a predominantly hyperdense collection. A linear fracture crosses the right temporal squamous bone. What is the single most important imaging feature to communicate urgently to the neurosurgical team in addition to the volume and midline shift?

A. The linear skull fracture  
B. The lentiform shape of the collection  
C. The swirl sign within the collection  
D. The temporal location of the haematoma  
E. The suture limitation of the collection

**Q2.** A 72-year-old woman on warfarin falls and hits the back of her head. CT shows a posterior fossa extra-axial collection measuring 11 mm in maximal thickness. It is lentiform, stops at the lambdoid suture, and is associated with compression of the 4th ventricle. Her GCS is 14. What is the most appropriate next step?

A. Discharge with head injury advice and GP follow-up  
B. Admit for observation only; repeat CT in 24 hours  
C. Urgent neurosurgical discussion given posterior fossa location and 4th ventricle compression  
D. Immediate contrast-enhanced CT to assess for enhancement  
E. CTA to identify active arterial extravasation before any escalation

**Q3.** On reviewing a CT brain in a 19-year-old after a road traffic collision, you identify an extra-axial collection adjacent to the right parietal convexity. It is crescentic in shape, appears hyperdense, and extends across the coronal suture. There is no adjacent fracture. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Extradural haematoma from middle meningeal artery injury  
B. Acute subdural haematoma  
C. Subgaleal haematoma  
D. Intra-diploic haematoma  
E. Extradural empyema

**Q4.** You are reviewing a non-contrast CT on a 58-year-old man who sustained a head injury 10 days ago and now re-presents with headache. There is a right-sided extra-axial collection with smooth lentiform morphology, sharp extra-axial margins, and an attenuation of approximately 35 HU, similar to grey matter. It stops at the coronal suture. There is mild sulcal effacement. What is the most helpful adjunct to confirm this is an evolving EDH rather than a normal variant or artefact?

A. Perform contrast-enhanced CT and look for peripheral nodular enhancement  
B. Request MRI brain with FLAIR sequences  
C. Compare with any prior imaging and assess for interval development in the correct clinical context  
D. Measure the collection density; if below 40 HU it cannot be a haematoma  
E. Discharge the patient as isodense extra-axial collections are always benign

**Q5.** A 7-year-old child is brought in after falling from a trampoline and striking the vertex of his head. Initial axial CT slices appear unremarkable. His GCS is 14 and he has a boggy scalp haematoma at the vertex. What is the most appropriate imaging action to avoid missing a significant intracranial injury?

A. No further imaging required as axial CT was normal  
B. Request an MRI brain as CT is unreliable in children  
C. Perform thin-slice coronal reconstructions of the vertex to assess for a parasagittal EDH  
D. Request a skull X-ray to look for a diastatic suture  
E. Repeat the axial CT with contrast to improve detection

---

### Answers

**Q1. C — The swirl sign within the collection**

The swirl sign (foci of lower attenuation within a hyperdense EDH) represents active, ongoing haemorrhage and reliably predicts haematoma expansion. It is the most critical dynamic feature to communicate urgently, over and above the already-threshold-exceeding volume and midline shift.

- **A (skull fracture)**: Important to document and implies MMA injury, but does not indicate active bleeding or predict expansion as directly as the swirl sign.
- **B (lentiform shape)**: Confirms the diagnosis but is a static morphological descriptor, not a dynamic risk feature.
- **D (temporal location)**: Relevant to herniation risk and should be noted, but the swirl sign is the more urgent actionable finding once thresholds are already breached.
- **E (suture limitation)**: Confirms the diagnosis as EDH rather than SDH; this is a diagnostic, not a prognostic or urgency-defining, feature.

---

**Q2. C — Urgent neurosurgical discussion given posterior fossa location and 4th ventricle compression**

Posterior fossa EDH carries disproportionate risk because the compartment is small and 4th ventricle compression can rapidly cause obstructive hydrocephalus and tonsillar herniation. Any posterior fossa EDH with neurological signs or thickness >10–12 mm warrants immediate neurosurgical discussion regardless of absolute volume.

- **A (discharge)**: Dangerous — this patient is on anticoagulation, has a reduced GCS, and has 4th ventricle compression.
- **B (observe, repeat in 24 hours)**: Too passive; the time frame is inappropriate given posterior fossa location and mass effect.
- **D (contrast-enhanced CT)**: EDH does not require contrast for diagnosis; this would delay necessary escalation.
- **E (CTA before escalation)**: CTA may have a role in planning but should not delay neurosurgical notification in this acutely deteriorating scenario.

---

**Q3. B — Acute subdural haematoma**

The collection is crescentic and crosses the coronal suture — both features exclude EDH, which is lentiform and stops at suture lines. Crossing a suture line is the defining differentiator here and points firmly to SDH.

- **A (EDH from MMA)**: MMA-related EDH is lentiform and does not cross suture lines; this collection does both.
- **C (subgaleal haematoma)**: A subgaleal collection lies outside the skull and would not cause intracranial mass effect; it appears on soft-tissue windows above the calvarium.
- **D (intra-diploic haematoma)**: Located within the diploic space and would expand both skull tables; bone windows would clarify.
- **E (extradural empyema)**: Would be associated with sinusitis, osteomyelitis, or sepsis; no such context is given, and acute empyema is not hyperdense in this way.

---

**Q4. C — Compare with prior imaging and assess for interval development in the correct clinical context**

When an EDH evolves to become isodense (approximately 30–40 HU at ~1–2 weeks), it loses conspicuity on HU alone. The lentiform shape, sharp extra-axial margins, and suture limitation remain the key structural clues. Correlation with prior imaging and clinical history (known head injury 10 days ago) is the most practical confirmatory step.

- **A (contrast CT for nodular enhancement)**: EDH does not enhance; thick nodular enhancement would suggest an alternative diagnosis. Thin peripheral dural enhancement can be reactive and is non-specific.
- **B (MRI with FLAIR)**: MRI can be valuable in complex cases but is not the first-line confirmatory step and may not be immediately available in an ED setting.
- **D (below 40 HU cannot be haematoma)**: Incorrect; evolving EDH passes through isodensity (~30–40 HU) as it resolves; this HU value does not exclude haematoma.
- **E (discharge as benign)**: Dangerous; any isodense extra-axial collection with mass effect in the correct clinical context requires further assessment, not discharge.

---

**Q5. C — Perform thin-slice coronal reconstructions of the vertex to assess for a parasagittal EDH**

Vertex EDH is a well-recognised pitfall on standard axial CT due to beam-hardening and partial volume artefact at the skull vault. Thin-slice coronal reconstructions are the essential next step to visualise parasagittal lentiform collections that may be invisible on axial imaging alone.

- **A (no further imaging)**: Axial CT at the vertex is unreliable for vertex EDH; a normal axial study does not exclude it.
- **B (MRI as CT unreliable in children)**: CT remains the primary acute modality; MRI is not the immediate solution in an ED setting for acute haemorrhage detection at the vertex.
- **D (skull X-ray)**: While sutural diastasis is a clue in children, plain radiography is insensitive for intracranial injury and should not replace CT reformats.
- **E (repeat axial CT with contrast)**: Contrast does not aid detection of acute haemorrhage and would not address the geometric limitation of axial plane imaging at the vertex.
