# A Systematic Approach to Reading Acute Brain CT
### Practical Guide for ED Physicians and Junior Radiologists

---

## 1. Clinical Context and Indications

Acute non-contrast CT (NCCT) is the first-line emergency neuroimaging tool. Know why the scan was requested before you start reading it. Common indications include focal neurological deficit, reduced consciousness, head trauma, thunderclap headache, seizures, and suspected anticoagulation-related bleeding.

Four questions must be answered on every scan:
1. Is there haemorrhage?
2. Is there established large infarct, mass effect, or herniation?
3. Is there a surgically remediable lesion (e.g., epidural haematoma, obstructive hydrocephalus)?
4. Are there findings that support or preclude reperfusion therapy?

---

## 2. CT Physics Essentials and Optimisation

### 2.1 Attenuation Values to Know

Recognising abnormality depends on knowing what is normal. Key approximate Hounsfield unit (HU) values:

| Tissue | HU |
|---|---|
| Air | −1000 |
| Fat | −100 to −50 |
| Water/CSF | 0–15 |
| White matter | <mark>20–30</mark> |
| Grey matter | <mark>30–40</mark> |
| Acute clot | <mark>50–80</mark> |
| Calcification | 80–200+ |
| Cortical bone | >1000 |

Always measure using a small region of interest (ROI), avoiding bone edges and partial volume. <mark>Compare any suspicious region to the contralateral homologous structure.</mark>

### 2.2 Window Settings

Using the wrong window is a common reason subtle findings are missed. Essential presets:

- **Brain window:** <mark>WL 35, WW 80</mark> — routine parenchymal review
- **Subdural/haemorrhage window:** <mark>WL 50–70, WW 130–150</mark> — improves detection of extra-axial collections
- **Posterior fossa window:** <mark>WL 35–40, WW 80–100</mark> — narrows width to counteract beam hardening
- **Bone window:** WL 300–500, WW 2500–4000

Always check patient demographics and scan laterality marker before interpreting.

## 3. A Systematic Search Pattern

Use a consistent **"outside-in, then inside-out"** approach, scrolling caudo-cranially. Never jump straight to the parenchyma.

### 3.1 Outside-In: Scalp, Skull, and Soft Tissues

- **Scalp:** haematomas, lacerations, subgaleal collections, gas (suggests open injury or infection)
- **Calvarium and skull base (bone windows):** linear, diastatic or depressed fractures; temporal bone and carotid canal fractures; anterior skull base for CSF leak risk
- **Orbits:** globe contour, lens position, retrobulbar haemorrhage, optic nerve sheath enlargement, blowout fractures
- **Sinuses and mastoids:** fluid or haemotympanum (supports basal skull fracture)

### 3.2 Extra-Axial Spaces

Examine the dural reflections (falx, tentorium) and all convexities. Look for:
- Hyperdense collections hugging the inner table
- Asymmetry of subarachnoid spaces
- Basal cistern effacement or hyperdensity (SAH)

### 3.3 Ventricular System

- Size and symmetry: <mark>temporal horn dilatation is an early sign of hydrocephalus</mark>
- Periventricular low attenuation "halo" = transependymal oedema — implies raised intraventricular pressure
- Intraventricular haemorrhage (IVH): hyperdense layering in occipital horns; "cast" configuration with heavy burden

### 3.4 Midline and Mass Effect

- Measure midline shift at the septum pellucidum and pineal gland
- Assess cistern patency (see Section 6)

### 3.5 Intra-Axial Parenchyma

Key targets:
- **Grey–white differentiation:** insular ribbon, lentiform nucleus outline, cortical ribbon
- **Vascular territories:** MCA (superior/inferior divisions), ACA, PCA, deep perforators (basal ganglia, internal capsule, thalami)
- **White matter:** distinguish leukoaraiosis (chronic, symmetric) from acute oedema (asymmetric, mass effect)

### 3.6 Vessels and Dural Sinuses

- Hyperdense artery signs: MCA M1 segment, basilar artery
- Dural sinuses (superior sagittal, transverse, sigmoid): dense sinus or "cord" sign for thrombosis
- Always use bone window to exclude calcification before calling a sinus hyperdense

### 3.7 Posterior Fossa

- Cerebellar hemispheres, vermis, brainstem contours, fourth ventricle
- <mark>Beam hardening from petrous bones degrades sensitivity here — use a narrow window and have a low threshold for CTA or MRI</mark>

## 4. Haemorrhage: Recognition, Mechanisms, Evolution

Acute clot is hyperdense because clotting concentrates haematocrit and protein. Over days to weeks, erythrocyte lysis reduces attenuation towards CSF density. Knowing this temporal evolution prevents missing subacute blood.

### 4.1 Intraparenchymal Haemorrhage (IPH)

**Attenuation:**
- Acute: <mark>50–80 HU</mark>, with peripheral hypoattenuating oedema rim (<mark>20–30 HU</mark>)
- Subacute (<mark>3–14 days</mark>): decreasing to near-isodense (<mark>35–45 HU</mark>)
- Chronic (<mark>>2–3 weeks</mark>): <mark>10–25 HU</mark>, may cavitate

**Location guides aetiology:**
- *Hypertensive:* deep locations — putamen, thalamus, pons, cerebellar dentate; rounded morphology; IVH extension common
- *Lobar/amyloid:* subcortical, irregular, possibly multiple
- *Haemorrhagic transformation of infarct:* conforms to an arterial territory; gyral pattern

**Expansion risk signs (act on these):**
- Heterogeneous density with hypoattenuating foci ("swirl sign," "black hole sign")
- "Blend sign" and irregular margins
- These predict haematoma expansion → escalate for blood pressure control, anticoagulation reversal, and consider haemostatic therapy

**Differentials:** calcified lesion (very high stable HU on bone windows), haemorrhagic tumour (disproportionate oedema, prior imaging comparison), post-contrast staining (very high HU, clinical context)

**When to escalate:** cerebellar haemorrhage with fourth ventricle effacement → urgent neurosurgical review; any IPH with mass effect or herniation signs.

### 4.2 Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)

**Recognition:**
- <mark>Acute SAH: 45–60+ HU</mark> in sulci and basal cisterns, following CSF spaces
- Aneurysmal SAH: basal cistern predominance with Sylvian extension
- Perimesencephalic SAH: confined around the midbrain; lower morbidity
- Traumatic SAH: convexity sulci near contusions

**Temporal sensitivity:**
- <mark>NCCT sensitivity >95% within 6 hours of ictus</mark>, declining over subsequent days as attenuation falls and CSF clears blood

**Critical pitfall — Pseudo-SAH:**
- Diffuse cerebral oedema lowers parenchymal attenuation, making engorged sulcal veins appear relatively hyperdense; look for globally effaced sulci and reduced overall brain attenuation. Use ROI measurement and clinical context.

**Management:** Aneurysmal SAH → urgent CTA, neurocritical care, BP control, nimodipine; assess for hydrocephalus requiring EVD. <mark>Modified Fisher grade correlates with vasospasm risk.</mark>

### 4.3 Subdural Haematoma (SDH)

**Morphology:** crescentic extra-axial collection, <mark>crosses sutures but is limited by dural reflections (falx, tentorium)</mark>

**Attenuation evolution:**
- Acute: <mark>50–80 HU</mark>
- Isodense (subacute): <mark>approximately 1–2 weeks post-ictus</mark>, attenuation <mark>30–40 HU</mark>
- Chronic: <mark>5–20 HU</mark>

**Key clue:** disproportionate mass effect for collection thickness; cortical buckling; fluid–fluid levels in anticoagulated patients

**Pitfall:** Isodense SDH in the subacute phase is easily missed — switch to subdural window and look for sulcal effacement and asymmetric ventricles.

**Management:** Large or symptomatic SDH → neurosurgical evacuation; anticoagulation reversal urgently.

### 4.4 Epidural (Extradural) Haematoma (EDH)

**Morphology:** lentiform biconvex collection, <mark>limited by cranial sutures</mark>; classically temporal with underlying skull fracture and lacerated middle meningeal artery

**Attenuation:** acute <mark>50–80 HU</mark>; hypoattenuating "swirl" foci within = active bleeding

**Pitfall:** vertex EDH may appear crescentic — use sagittal reformats.

**Management:** Surgical emergency if mass effect or herniation signs present; monitor closely even if initially stable.

### 4.5 Intraventricular Haemorrhage (IVH)

- Hyperdense dependent layering in occipital horns; "cast" if heavy
- Identify source: extension from IPH, aneurysm, trauma, choroid plexus lesion
- Key complication: obstructive hydrocephalus requiring EVD

## 5. Acute Ischaemia and Infarction

Cytotoxic oedema begins within minutes of arterial occlusion, shifting water intracellularly and reducing attenuation by only a few HU — changes that are subtle and require active search.

### 5.1 Early NCCT Signs (0–6 Hours)

These are the most clinically critical findings:

- **Loss of grey–white differentiation:**
  - *Insular ribbon sign:* blurred insular cortex (earliest and most reliable sign in MCA territory)
  - *Lentiform nucleus obscuration:* loss of basal ganglia definition
  - *Cortical ribbon hypodensity* with sulcal effacement
  - <mark>Typical early attenuation drop: 2–6 HU versus the contralateral side</mark>

- **Hyperdense artery sign:**
  - <mark>MCA M1 absolute HU frequently >43–47 HU and >10 HU asymmetry versus contralateral increases specificity</mark>
  - Dense basilar sign: <mark>critical to recognise in any patient presenting with reduced GCS</mark>

- **Territory and extent:**
  - <mark>Hypodensity involving >1/3 of the MCA territory correlates with large infarct core and higher haemorrhagic transformation risk</mark>

### 5.2 Evolution (6 Hours to Days)

- 6–24 hours: increasing hypoattenuation (<mark>15–25 HU</mark>), swelling, early petechial haemorrhagic transformation
- 3–7 days: maximal oedema and midline shift
- Subacute/chronic: volume loss, gliosis (attenuation approaching CSF), ex vacuo dilatation

### 5.3 ASPECTS (Anterior Circulation)

The **Alberta Stroke Programme Early CT Score** grades early ischaemic change in the MCA territory.

- <mark>10 defined regions: C (caudate), L (lentiform), IC (internal capsule), I (insular ribbon), M1–M6 (cortical MCA zones)</mark>
- <mark>Start at 10; subtract 1 point for each region showing hypoattenuation or loss of grey–white differentiation</mark>
- <mark>ASPECTS <6 suggests large infarct core — thrombectomy eligibility follows local extended criteria pathways</mark>

**Practical tip:** Use narrow windows (<mark>WL 35–40, WW 30–40</mark>) to accentuate grey–white contrast differences. Cross-check with clinical lateralisation.

### 5.4 Posterior Circulation Stroke

Subtle cerebellar or brainstem hypoattenuation with fourth ventricle effacement; beam hardening severely limits CT sensitivity. <mark>Have a low threshold for CTA or MRI when posterior circulation stroke is suspected clinically, even if CT appears normal.</mark>

### 5.5 Key Differentials of Hypoattenuation

| Finding | Key distinguishing features |
|---|---|
| Chronic infarct | Well-defined, volume loss, ex vacuo dilatation, no mass effect |
| Leukoaraiosis | Periventricular/subcortical, symmetric, spares insular ribbon and cortex |
| PRES | Symmetric parieto-occipital white matter predominance; hypertensive context |
| Post-ictal change | Not conforming to vascular territory; typically transient |
| Hypoglycaemic injury | Bilateral symmetric cortex/deep nuclei — check glucose immediately |

## 6. Mass Effect and Herniation Syndromes

Assess cisterns and midline shift on every scan. Recognise the pattern, not just the presence, of herniation.

| Syndrome | CT findings | Clinical implication |
|---|---|---|
| Subfalcine | Cingulate gyrus under falx; midline shift | ACA compression → risk of ACA infarct |
| Uncal (transtentorial) | Medial temporal lobe displacing midbrain; effaced ipsilateral ambient cistern | CN III palsy; risk of contralateral PCA infarct |
| Central downward | Effaced suprasellar/perimesencephalic cisterns; small third ventricle | Progressive brainstem compression |
| Tonsillar | Cerebellar tonsils crowding foramen magnum; effaced premedullary cistern | Cardiorespiratory compromise |
| Transcalvarial | Herniation through skull defect | Post-craniectomy context |

**Management:** Any herniation sign → immediate neurocritical measures (head elevation, osmotherapy, hyperventilation as bridge) and urgent neurosurgical consultation.

## 7. Cerebral Venous Thrombosis (CVT) on NCCT

CVT is frequently missed because findings are indirect and non-specific.

**Direct signs:**
- <mark>Dense sinus sign: hyperdense dural sinus or cortical vein (50–70+ HU)</mark>; compare to ipsilateral arteries and contralateral sinus
- Beware: dehydration and polycythaemia can increase blood density and simulate this sign

**Indirect signs:**
- Venous infarcts: subcortical oedema or haemorrhage <mark>not conforming to arterial vascular territories</mark>
- Parasagittal or bitemporal distribution for superior sagittal sinus thrombosis
- Bilateral or asymmetric gyral swelling

**Pitfalls:** Physiological sinus asymmetry; sinus calcification (very high HU, sharply marginated on bone windows).

**Action:** Low threshold for CT venography if any suspicion. Treatment is anticoagulation unless contraindicated.

## 8. Common Pitfalls and Artefacts

| Pitfall | How to avoid it |
|---|---|
| Beam hardening (posterior fossa) | Narrow window, thin slices, coronal reformats; low threshold for MRI |
| Pseudo-SAH | Look for globally low brain HU and diffuse sulcal effacement; measure ROI |
| Isodense subacute SDH | Use subdural window; search for asymmetric sulcal effacement and ventricular shift |
| Calcification vs haemorrhage | Check bone window; stable very high HU; no surrounding oedema |
| Dehydration/high haematocrit | May simulate hyperdense artery sign — use asymmetry, not absolute density alone |
| Post-contrast staining | Very high HU, clinical history of recent angiography; consider dual-energy CT |
| Vertex/skull base collections | Use multiplanar reformats to characterise |
| Dental/hardware streak | Consider gantry tilt; correlate with clinical brainstem findings via MRI |

## 9. Structured Differential Diagnoses

### Hyperdense extra-axial lesion
- **EDH:** biconvex, <mark>suture-limited</mark>, adjacent fracture, 50–80 HU
- **SDH:** crescentic, <mark>crosses sutures</mark>, variable density with age, may have membranes/fluid levels
- **Meningioma:** iso- to slightly hyperdense; calcification; no acute hyperdensity unless haemorrhagic

### Hyperdensity in sulci/cisterns
- **SAH:** follows CSF spaces; basal or perimesencephalic distribution
- **Pseudo-SAH:** globally low parenchymal HU; effaced sulci; anoxic context
- **Subarachnoid contrast:** very high HU; recent procedure history

### Hyperdense intra-axial focus
- **Acute IPH:** 50–80 HU with oedema rim
- **Calcification:** very high HU, sharply marginated, no oedema
- **Cavernoma:** "popcorn" morphology, calcification, often multiple
- **Haemorrhagic metastasis:** lobar, disproportionate oedema, multiplicity

### Hypoattenuating parenchymal change
- **Acute infarct:** territorial, grey–white loss, evolving mass effect
- **Chronic infarct:** volume loss, gliosis, no mass effect
- **PRES:** parieto-occipital white matter predominance, symmetric
- **HSV encephalitis:** medial temporal/insular low density — subtle on CT, MRI preferred

## 10. Reporting: A Pragmatic Template

A structured report ensures nothing is missed and aids clinical teams in making urgent decisions.

1. **Technique/quality:** motion, artefact, windows used
2. **Extra-cranial:** scalp, skull fractures, orbits, sinuses, mastoids
3. **Extra-axial spaces:** SDH/EDH/SAH; cistern patency
4. **Ventricles/CSF:** size, symmetry, hydrocephalus, transependymal oedema, IVH
5. **Parenchyma:** haemorrhage (location, size, HU), infarction (territory, ASPECTS, mass effect)
6. **Vessels/sinuses:** hyperdense artery/sinus signs
7. **Mass effect/herniation:** midline shift in mm, cisternal effacement, herniation pattern
8. **Impression:** concise and management-relevant

> *Example:* "No haemorrhage. Early right MCA ischaemic changes, ASPECTS 7, hyperdense M1 sign — urgent CTA for thrombectomy pathway."
> *Example:* "Large left cerebellar haemorrhage with fourth ventricle effacement — urgent neurosurgical review."

## 11. Clinical and Management Correlations

- **Thrombolysis/thrombectomy:** Exclude haemorrhage; quantify early ischaemic change (ASPECTS); hyperdense artery sign → expedite CTA
- **Anticoagulation status:** Guides reversal in ICH; explains fluid–fluid levels in haematomas
- **Blood pressure:** Aggressive control in ICH; cautious management in acute ischaemic stroke
- **Neurosurgical triggers:** EDH with mass effect; posterior fossa haemorrhage with fourth ventricle effacement; malignant MCA oedema with herniation; obstructive hydrocephalus (EVD)

---

## 12. High-Yield Pre-Read Checklist

Before reading, confirm: patient identity, time of symptom onset, anticoagulation status, clinical lateralisation of deficit.

On every scan, actively assess:
- [ ] Basal cisterns — patent? blood?
- [ ] Ventricles — size? temporal horns? IVH?
- [ ] Midline shift in mm; falx and tentorium integrity
- [ ] Grey–white differentiation at insula, lentiform, cortex
- [ ] Hyperdense artery sign (MCA, basilar)
- [ ] Extra-axial spaces at convexities, falx, tentorium
- [ ] Posterior fossa with narrow window
- [ ] Skull base and sinuses on bone windows

---

## 13. Take-Home Points

- Use a disciplined outside-in search pattern every time. Adjust windows strategically.
- <mark>Acute haemorrhage is typically 50–80 HU</mark>, but evolution and mixing change appearance — recognise the subacute isodense phase.
- <mark>Early infarct attenuation change may be only 2–6 HU</mark> — active search in the insula and lentiform nucleus is essential.
- <mark>NCCT sensitivity for SAH exceeds 95% within 6 hours</mark> but falls rapidly thereafter.
- <mark>Hypodensity >1/3 of MCA territory and ASPECTS <6 indicate large infarct core</mark> — know your local reperfusion criteria.
- Herniation is a clinical emergency: recognise the cistern pattern, escalate immediately.
- Correlate everything with the clinical timeline — the same CT appearance at 1 hour and 3 days has entirely different implications.

## Self-assessment MCQs

**Q1.** A 68-year-old man is brought to the ED with sudden left-sided weakness and dysphasia. His NCCT is performed 90 minutes after onset. On brain windows, there is subtle loss of the right insular ribbon and indistinctness of the right lentiform nucleus. The right MCA M1 segment appears hyperdense. What is the single most appropriate immediate next step?

A. Administer IV alteplase immediately without further imaging
B. Arrange urgent CT angiography to assess for large vessel occlusion
C. Repeat the NCCT in 6 hours to confirm evolving infarction
D. Arrange MRI brain with DWI as the hyperdense MCA sign is non-specific
E. Perform lumbar puncture to exclude subarachnoid haemorrhage

**Q2.** A 45-year-old woman presents with thunderclap headache. NCCT is performed 8 hours after onset and reported as normal. Which statement most accurately describes the appropriate next step?

A. Discharge with analgesia as a normal CT at 8 hours reliably excludes subarachnoid haemorrhage
B. Perform CT angiography as a normal NCCT excludes parenchymal pathology but not aneurysm
C. Perform lumbar puncture or CT angiography, as NCCT sensitivity for SAH declines significantly after 6 hours
D. Repeat NCCT at 24 hours when blood will be more conspicuous
E. Arrange MRI FLAIR sequences, which are more sensitive than NCCT at all time points after SAH

**Q3.** A 55-year-old man with atrial fibrillation on warfarin presents with confusion after a minor fall. NCCT shows a right-sided crescentic extra-axial collection. The collection appears isodense to brain parenchyma. There is subtle effacement of right cortical sulci and 4 mm leftward midline shift. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the most important immediate management priority?

A. Right epidural haematoma; arrange urgent CT angiography
B. Right chronic subdural haematoma; reassure and arrange outpatient follow-up
C. Right subacute subdural haematoma (isodense phase); reverse anticoagulation and refer to neurosurgery
D. Pseudo-SAH from cerebral oedema; treat underlying cause and manage ICP
E. Right subdural empyema; commence intravenous antibiotics and arrange MRI

**Q4.** You are reviewing an NCCT of a 72-year-old with acute-onset left hemiplegia. Applying the ASPECTS scoring system to the right MCA territory, you identify hypoattenuation in the right insular ribbon, lentiform nucleus, and M2 and M3 cortical zones. What is the ASPECTS score, and what is the clinical implication of this finding?

A. ASPECTS 6; large infarct core — discuss with thrombectomy team using extended criteria
B. ASPECTS 4; large infarct core — likely outside standard thrombectomy criteria
C. ASPECTS 8; mild early changes — patient suitable for standard thrombectomy assessment
D. ASPECTS 6; finding excludes thrombolysis but does not affect thrombectomy eligibility
E. ASPECTS 8; large infarct core — patient should not receive any reperfusion therapy

**Q5.** A 30-year-old woman presents with progressive headache, papilloedema, and a new focal seizure. NCCT shows a right parasagittal cortical hypodense area with petechial haemorrhage that does not correspond to a standard arterial territory. The superior sagittal sinus appears hyperdense compared with adjacent brain. What is the most likely diagnosis and the appropriate management?

A. Right MCA territory infarction with haemorrhagic transformation; expedite CTA for thrombectomy
B. Right cerebral abscess with rupture; neurosurgical drainage and antibiotics
C. Cerebral venous thrombosis of the superior sagittal sinus; anticoagulate unless contraindicated
D. Hypertensive intraparenchymal haemorrhage; control blood pressure and neurosurgical review
E. Right hemisphere glioblastoma with haemorrhage; arrange MRI with contrast

---

### Answers

**Q1. B**
*Correct:* The combination of early ischaemic signs and a hyperdense MCA M1 sign indicates likely large vessel occlusion; urgent CTA is required to confirm this and activate the thrombectomy pathway — this must happen before or alongside any thrombolysis decision.
- *A:* Thrombolysis without CTA would miss the opportunity for thrombectomy and requires formal imaging and clinical eligibility assessment first.
- *C:* Delaying repeat imaging wastes the treatment time window and is inappropriate.
- *D:* MRI DWI is more sensitive for early infarct but CTA is the immediate priority to evaluate for LVO and guide thrombectomy; MRI would cause harmful delay.
- *E:* LP is used to detect SAH when CT is negative; it is not indicated here where haemorrhage has already been excluded and ischaemia is the primary concern.

**Q2. C**
*Correct:* NCCT sensitivity for SAH falls significantly after 6 hours from ictus; a normal scan at 8 hours does not exclude SAH and LP (to detect xanthochromia) or CTA is required.
- *A:* Discharging on the basis of a normal CT at 8 hours risks missing a significant proportion of SAH cases and is unsafe.
- *B:* CTA evaluates for aneurysm but does not substitute for confirming whether SAH has occurred; the issue here is diagnosis of haemorrhage, not aetiology alone.
- *D:* Blood attenuation decreases over time, not increases — a repeat CT later would be less sensitive, not more.
- *E:* FLAIR MRI is more sensitive than NCCT in the subacute period but is not universally available acutely; LP or CTA remains the standard next step in most emergency pathways.

**Q3. C**
*Correct:* An isodense crescentic extra-axial collection with mass effect and midline shift in an anticoagulated patient represents a subacute SDH in its isodense phase (approximately 1–2 weeks old); reversal of anticoagulation is urgent and neurosurgical referral is required given the mass effect.
- *A:* EDH is biconvex and limited by sutures; a crescentic collection is characteristic of SDH. CTA is not the immediate priority.
- *B:* There is significant mass effect and midline shift; this is not appropriate for outpatient follow-up.
- *D:* Pseudo-SAH involves apparent sulcal hyperdensity due to low parenchymal attenuation; this presentation describes an extra-axial collection, not this artefact.
- *E:* Subdural empyema is possible but would be expected in a febrile patient; the attenuation characteristics and anticoagulation context make subacute SDH far more likely.

**Q4. A**
*Correct:* Four regions are affected (insular ribbon, lentiform nucleus, M2, M3), giving ASPECTS = 10 − 4 = 6, indicating a significant infarct core; this should prompt discussion with the thrombectomy team as some extended criteria protocols may still offer treatment.
- *B:* The score is 6 (10 − 4), not 4; ASPECTS 4 would require six affected regions.
- *C:* ASPECTS 8 would require only two affected regions; four are identified here.
- *D:* ASPECTS 6 does not automatically exclude thrombectomy; eligibility depends on local protocols and extended criteria.
- *E:* ASPECTS 8 is incorrect for four affected regions; furthermore, ASPECTS 8 would suggest a small core suitable for standard reperfusion assessment, not exclusion from therapy.

**Q5. C**
*Correct:* A parasagittal cortical lesion with haemorrhage not conforming to an arterial territory, combined with a hyperdense superior sagittal sinus, is the classic CT presentation of cerebral venous thrombosis; anticoagulation is the treatment of choice and should not be withheld solely because of haemorrhage.
- *A:* MCA infarction follows a standard arterial territory; parasagittal involvement crossing territories with a hyperdense sinus strongly favours CVT rather than arterial occlusion.
- *B:* Abscess would typically appear as a ring-enhancing lesion on contrast CT with a defined capsule; the hyperdense sinus and territorial distribution make CVT the correct diagnosis.
- *D:* Hypertensive haemorrhage typically occurs in deep structures (putamen, thalamus, pons) in an older patient; this parasagittal cortical pattern in a young woman with a hyperdense sinus is not consistent.
- *E:* Glioblastoma is possible in isolation but does not explain the hyperdense sinus; the combination of findings, young age, and seizure/headache presentation makes CVT the single best answer.
